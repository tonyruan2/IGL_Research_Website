Monotonicty vs Periodicity

in the

Law of Large Numbers

Project Overview

Suppose we have a process that succeeds X% amount of time and all experiments are independent. Let’s call X the process probability.

We want to achieve at least Y% success when running the process over a set number of trials N. Let’s call Y the desired probability, and assume Y is rational. That is, Y can be written as a over b, where a and b are integers.

What value of N will give us the greatest probability to achieve at least our desired probability?

The Law of Large Numbers tells us three possible behaviors as N tends to infinity.

  • If our process probability is less than our desired probability, we will have a 0% chance to reach our desired probability.

  • If our process probability is equal to our desired probability, we will have a 50% chance to reach our desired probability.

  • If our process probability is greater than our desired probability, we will have a 100% chance to reach our desired probability.

But suppose we cannot run the process a large amount of times. That is, suppose the process is costly to run and requires a substantial amount of resources.

Let’s limit the number of trials N to a positive integer T, which is less than or equal to 1000. In other words, assume we have the resources to run between 1 and T trials.

Then what should we choose N to be to maximize our probability to achieve our desired probability? Alternatively, what should we choose N to be to minimize our probability to achieve our desired probability?

It turns out that there are certain monotonic and periodic behaviors related to the trial count and the probability to achieve our desired probability.

The goal of our project is to investigate these monotonic and periodic behaviors in the mathematical scenario described.
