Findings: a general overview

Finding 1

Observation

In the long run, there is a certain overall trend in the probability to achieve at least the desired probability.

Conjecture

In the long run, there are three general behaviors:

  • If our process probability is less than our desired probability, the resulting probabilities converge to 0 and the overall trend is decreasing.
  • If our process probability is equal to our desired probability, the resulting probabilities converge to 0.5 and the overall trend is increasing below 0.5 and decreasing above 0.5.
  • If our process probability is greater than our desired probability, the resulting probabilities converge to 1 and the overall trend is increasing.

Investigation

This conjecture is true by the Law of Large Numbers.

Significance

We can use the long run behavior to estimate the optimal trial counts for the maximum and minimum probabilities of success.

If the long run behavior is decreasing, we can hypothetize that smaller trial counts will offer greater probabilities of success than larger trial counts.

If the long run behavior is increasing, we can hypothetize that larger trial counts will offer greater probabilities of success than smaller trial counts.

If the long run behavior is a convergence to 0.5, we can hypothetize that some small trial count will offer the optimal probability of success.

Finding 2

Observation

There appears to be distinct ‘lines’ of trial counts in which the probability to achieve at least the desired probability converges in a monotone manner.

Conjecture

For all trial counts equivalent mod B, the probability to achieve at least the desired probability has a monotonic behavior.

Investigation

This conjecture is false.

As seen in the image, there is an initial decrease in probability followed by an increase in probability for both the blue and purple groups of trial counts. This means the behavior is not monotone.

Revised Conjecture

For all trial counts equivalent mod B, the probability to achieve at least the desired probability has a monotonic behavior for a trial count n that is greater than or equal to k, where k is a sufficiently large positive integer.

In other words, there is always monotonic long run behavior (but not short run behavior) for the probabilities of trial counts equivalent mod B.

Here, if we pick an integer k and set it equal to 20, we see that the probabilities of each equivalent mod group are monotonically increasing for trial counts greater than or equal to k.

Here, if we pick an integer k and set it equal to 30, we see that the probabilities of each equivalent mod group are monotonically decreasing for trial counts greater than or equal to k.

Significance

We can use the long run relationship in each mod equivalent group to further hypothesize which trial counts are optimal for the maximum and minimum probabilities of success.

Finding 3

Observation

Connecting the probabilities of trial counts equivalent mod B shows the greatest line of probabilities consists of the trial counts equal to 0 mod B. In other words, multiples of B appear to have the greatest probabilities.

Conjecture

For trial counts in [wB, (w+1)B], where w is an integer greater than 1, the trial count with the maximum probability is:

  • wB if the overall trend is decreasing or both decreasing and increasing (process < desired OR process == desired)
  • (w+1)B if the overall trend is increasing (process > desired)

Investigation

This conjecture is true. Note that the conjecture does not hold if w = 0. This is because a trial count of 0 is trivial.

Significance

We’ve found a way to find the trial count that provides the maximum probability of success for a range [wB, (w+1)B].

Finding 4

Observation

There appears to be a repeating pattern in the probabilities for each consecutive group of B trials.

Conjecture

Every B trials, counting implicitly from 0 or explicitly from 1, the probabilities exhibit a periodic behavior.

Investigation

This conjecture is true.

Significance

We can use the pattern of the first or second group of B trials to model the behavior of probabilities in the long run.

The pattern reveals an ordering among a group of B trial counts, and this ordering can be extended to other groups of B trial counts (which means all other trial counts).

Finding 5

Observation

There are subpatterns in the periodic behavior of the probabilities.

Significance

These subpatterns tell us the exact ordering of trial counts relative to one another.

These subpatterns, along with the long run behavior, reveal the optimal trial count for the maximum and minimum probabilities of success over a sequential range of trial counts.
